## 1. Positioning Statement

BaxterLabs Advisory is a fixed-scope profit leak diagnostic for professional service firms generating $5M to $50M in revenue. It's not a retainer. It's not an ongoing advisory relationship. It's not a six-month engagement that costs six figures. It's a 14-day, $12,500 diagnostic that produces board-ready output with full citation provenance, identifies $200K to $1M in recoverable profit, and delivers a specific roadmap to fix what we found. The methodology carries the rigor of a Big 4 engagement (documented phases, repeatable process, audit-grade artifacts) at a fraction of the price and a fraction of the timeline, because the 8-phase pipeline is AI-assisted and the scope is fixed before day one. Two partners, George DeVries and Alfonso Cordon, deliver every engagement personally. No army of junior analysts. No scope creep. No ambiguity.

## 2. Matrix A: Depth of Analysis vs. Speed of Delivery

**Y Axis**: Depth of Analysis (low at bottom, high at top)
**X Axis**: Speed of Delivery (slow at left, fast at right)

### Quadrant Layout

| | Slow | Fast |
|---|---|---|
| **High Depth** | Upper-Left: Big 4 Consulting | Upper-Right: BaxterLabs Advisory |
| **Low Depth** | Lower-Left: Status Quo | Lower-Right: Fractional CFO |

### Player Placements

**BaxterLabs Advisory (Upper-Right: High Depth, Fast Delivery)**
The 8-phase pipeline produces a full diagnostic with quantified findings across all five canonical profit leak categories, a workbook with every number sourced and auditable, and a specific roadmap to fix what we found. That's the depth. The 14-day fixed window is the speed, enforced by AI-assisted delivery that compresses what a traditional firm takes 90 days to produce into two weeks. High depth and fast delivery occupy the same quadrant because the methodology was built from the ground up to do both simultaneously.

**Big 4 Consulting (Upper-Left: High Depth, Slow Delivery)**
Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG, and their tier-2 equivalents (Grant Thornton, RSM) produce rigorous, enterprise-grade deliverables when they run margin improvement engagements in the $5M to $50M segment. The depth is real. The methodology is documented, the teams are large, and the output is defensible. But the timeline is 3 to 6 months and the cost is $150K to $500K, because the delivery model is process-heavy, committee-driven, and built for Fortune 500 clients. A $25M professional service firm gets the same process that a $2.5B firm gets, which means the depth comes with a speed penalty the mid-market firm doesn't need to pay.

**Fractional CFO (Lower-Right: Low Depth, Fast Delivery)**
A fractional CFO is fast because the relationship is continuous. They're embedded in the business, typically at $3K to $8K per month, and they can act on something the same week they see it. But the depth of analysis is shallow relative to a forensic diagnostic. A fractional CFO is doing operational finance work: managing cash flow, producing monthly closes, overseeing AP/AR, supporting the managing partner on day-to-day decisions. They're not running an 8-phase diagnostic with citation provenance and quantified findings across five categories. The speed is real, but it's speed applied to a different kind of work.

**Status Quo (Lower-Left: Low Depth, Slow Delivery)**
No diagnostic is happening. The firm operates on the managing partner's intuition, monthly P&L reviews, and occasional conversations about why margin feels off. There's no structured analysis, so depth is zero. And there's no delivery timeline because nothing is being delivered. The managing partner might eventually notice a profit leak through a chance conversation or a line item that catches their eye, but that's not a methodology. It's luck. Meanwhile, $200K to $1M in recoverable profit stays leaked, year after year, because the standard month-end close is built to record what happened, not explain why.

## 3. Matrix B: Enterprise Rigor vs. Accessibility / Price

**Y Axis**: Enterprise Rigor (low at bottom, high at top)
**X Axis**: Accessibility / Price (expensive at left, accessible at right)

### Quadrant Layout

| | Expensive | Accessible |
|---|---|---|
| **High Rigor** | Upper-Left: Big 4 Consulting | Upper-Right: BaxterLabs Advisory |
| **Low Rigor** | Lower-Left (origin): Status Quo | Lower-Right: Fractional CFO |

### Player Placements

**BaxterLabs Advisory (Upper-Right: High Rigor, Accessible Price)**
The diagnostic produces audit-grade artifacts: a quantification workbook with every figure sourced and traceable, a classification system (Verified, Stated, Estimated, Derived) for every claim, and deliverables formatted for board presentation. That's enterprise rigor. The price is $12,500, which sits inside a mid-market firm's discretionary operating budget, not its capital budget. A managing partner can approve $12,500 without a board vote, a procurement process, or a six-month evaluation cycle. The combination of enterprise rigor and accessible price is the positioning that neither the Big 4 nor the fractional CFO can replicate, because the Big 4 can't deliver rigor without enterprise pricing, and the fractional CFO can't deliver rigor at all.

**Big 4 Consulting (Upper-Left: High Rigor, Expensive)**
The rigor is genuine. Big 4 firms have documented methodologies, institutional quality controls, and deliverables that stand up to board-level scrutiny. But the price reflects the enterprise delivery model: $150K to $500K for a margin improvement engagement in this segment. For a $25M professional service firm, that's 0.6% to 2% of revenue spent on the diagnostic alone, before a single recommendation is implemented. The rigor is worth paying for. The question is whether the mid-market firm needs to pay enterprise prices to get it.

**Fractional CFO (Lower-Right: Low Rigor, Accessible Price)**
This is where the differentiation between BaxterLabs and the fractional CFO matters most. Both sit on the right side of the X axis. A fractional CFO at $3K to $8K per month is accessible, and a managing partner who's already paying for one will reasonably ask why they need a $12,500 diagnostic on top of it. The answer is the Y axis. A fractional CFO is one person with personal frameworks, not an institutional methodology. The work product is operational finance output (cash flow forecasts, budget-to-actuals, vendor management) rather than a forensic diagnostic with citation provenance and quantified findings. The fractional CFO's value is continuous embedded support. BaxterLabs' value is a one-time forensic look that surfaces what continuous operations can't see. They're complementary, not competitive, but they sit in different quadrants because the rigor dimension separates them.

**Status Quo (At the Origin: No Rigor, No Direct Spend)**
Status Quo sits at the origin of the matrix. On the Y axis, there's no rigor because there's no methodology. On the X axis, direct spend is zero, which puts it at the accessible end of the price dimension in purely monetary terms. But this is where the matrix measurement and the actual economics diverge. The hidden cost of Status Quo is the $200K to $1M in recoverable profit that stays leaked year after year because nobody is looking for it with a structured methodology. The matrix can't show that cost directly because the X axis measures dollars out the door, not opportunity cost of inaction. The narrative has to carry that point separately. The managing partner's intuition about where margin is leaking might be right (it usually is), but without a diagnostic to confirm and quantify it, the intuition stays unactionable.

## 5. Objection Handling

### "Why not just hire Deloitte?"

You can. If you have $150K to $500K in budget and 3 to 6 months to wait for the output, a Big 4 firm will produce rigorous work. The question is whether you need to spend that much and wait that long to get the same quality of answer. The BaxterLabs diagnostic uses the same analytical discipline (documented methodology, citation provenance, board-ready deliverables) and typically surfaces $200K to $1M in recoverable profit. The difference is it costs $12,500 and takes 14 days. For a $25M professional service firm, the Big 4 option isn't wrong. It's just overbuilt for the problem.

### "We already have a fractional CFO."

Good. A fractional CFO is doing important work: managing cash flow, producing your monthly close, keeping the financial operations running. That's continuous embedded support, and it's valuable. What a fractional CFO typically doesn't do is run a forensic diagnostic across all five profit leak categories with quantified findings and citation provenance. It's a different kind of look. I think of it the same way you'd think about a general practitioner and a specialist. Your fractional CFO is the GP keeping the practice healthy month to month. The diagnostic is the specialist visit that surfaces what the regular checkups aren't designed to find. The two are complementary. Most of the fractional CFOs we've talked with are glad to have the diagnostic findings because it gives them specific, quantified items to work on implementing.

### "We're handling it internally, thanks."

I hear that a lot, and it makes sense. You know your business better than anyone. The question I'd ask is whether your current finance infrastructure is built to surface profit leaks, or built to report what already happened. Most firms in the $5M to $50M range rely on the monthly P&L and the managing partner's instinct. Both are useful. But the standard month-end close is designed to record what happened, not explain why margin is leaking. The diagnostic is a different kind of look. If your instinct says margin should be better than it is, that instinct is almost always right. The diagnostic is the thing that proves it right and puts a dollar figure on it. The minimum we typically find is $200K. If the diagnostic finds even one profit leak worth $12,500, it's paid for itself. The rest is recovered margin.

## 6. Risks to the Positioning

**"Accessible" is relative.** $12,500 is accessible compared to $150K. It's not accessible compared to zero. Some prospects in the $5M to $10M range will see $12,500 as a meaningful spend, especially if they haven't done a diagnostic before and aren't sure what they'll get. The fixed-scope, fixed-price framing helps (the buyer knows the total cost before day one), but case studies showing specific recoverable profit figures from completed engagements will be the strongest proof that the investment pays for itself. Until we have 5 to 10 published case studies, "accessible" is a credible claim but not yet a proven one.

**"Enterprise rigor" needs proof artifacts.** The methodology is documented, the phases are repeatable, and the deliverables carry citation provenance. But a prospect who hasn't seen the output is taking our word for it. The positioning claim is only as strong as the sample deliverables, anonymized case studies, and testimonials we can show during the sales process. Building a library of proof artifacts (redacted executive summaries, sample workbook pages, before-and-after margin comparisons) is the single biggest marketing priority for the next 90 days.

**The fractional CFO category is evolving.** Some fractional CFO firms are building more structured diagnostic offerings that sit on top of their ongoing retainer relationships. If a fractional CFO firm develops a repeatable, methodology-driven diagnostic product (rather than relying on individual personal frameworks), the lower-right quadrant of Matrix B starts moving upward. The defense is speed and depth: the BaxterLabs 8-phase pipeline is purpose-built for forensic diagnostics and produces output in 14 days that a fractional CFO firm building a diagnostic offering would need months to replicate. But this is worth monitoring quarterly, especially among fractional CFO networks that serve the same $5M to $50M professional service segment.
